Special Containment Procedures:

Full containment of SCP-2953 is currently impossible. Foundation efforts are focused on identifying affected individuals and providing appropriate psychological intervention.

All licensed psychiatrists and clinical psychologists within Foundation reach have been provided with Protocol 2953-Differentiate, allowing them to distinguish between actual schizophrenic disorder and SCP-2953 effects. Mental health professionals are not to be informed of the anomalous nature of the condition but are instead told it represents a newly discovered psychological phenomenon requiring specialized treatment.

Research into a method of preventing SCP-2953 transmission is ongoing at Site-19.

Description:

SCP-2953 is a self-propagating cognitohazard that causes affected individuals to become absolutely convinced they have schizophrenic disorder despite showing no actual symptoms of the condition. The cognitohazard appears to spread through prolonged discussion about schizophrenic disorders or through exposure to detailed descriptions of the condition in clinical settings.

The primary effect of SCP-2953 is a persistent and unshakeable belief that one is experiencing schizophrenic symptoms, particularly auditory hallucinations and paranoid delusions. What makes SCP-2953 distinct is that affected individuals do not actually experience any hallucinations, delusions, or other symptoms characteristic of schizophrenic disorder. Brain scans, psychological evaluations, and behavioral observations all confirm normal cognitive function.

However, subjects will adamantly insist they are hearing voices, seeing things that aren't there, or experiencing thought insertion, despite all evidence to the contrary. When questioned about specific hallucinations, subjects often provide vague, inconsistent, or textbook descriptions that appear to be based on their understanding of what schizophrenic disorder "should" be like rather than genuine experiences.

Most notably, subjects will frequently:

  • Claim to be hearing voices "right now" while showing none of the attentional shifts, pauses, or responses typically observed in actual auditory hallucinations
  • Report that they are experiencing bizarre delusions but be able to reason normally about these same beliefs when engaged in unrelated conversations
  • Describe their symptoms using terminology and concepts clearly derived from medical literature rather than personal experience
  • Express frustration when others do not acknowledge their "obvious" symptoms

SCP-2953 appears to be transmitted when an unaffected individual engages in detailed discussion about the nature of schizophrenic disorders with an affected individual for more than approximately 30 minutes. The probability of transmission increases significantly if the unaffected individual is already concerned about their mental health or has a family history of mental illness.

Addendum 2953-1: Case Studies

Case #1: Subject A, a 34-year-old professor of psychology, began insisting he had developed schizophrenic disorder after teaching a seminar on psychotic disorders. Despite continuing to function normally in his academic position and personal life, Subject A sought multiple psychiatric opinions, each time describing textbook symptoms that were inconsistent with his observed behavior. When one psychiatrist suggested he might not have schizophrenic disorder, Subject A became distressed and accused the doctor of "gaslighting" him. Foundation intervention with Protocol 2953-Differentiate resulted in successful treatment after 6 weeks.

Case #2: Subject B, a 28-year-old woman with no prior psychiatric history, developed SCP-2953 after her roommate (Subject C) was affected. Subject B reported to emergency services that she was "hearing voices telling me to hurt myself," yet showed no behavioral indications of responding to internal stimuli. When asked to describe the voices in detail, Subject B provided inconsistent accounts and admitted she "couldn't actually hear them with [her] ears" but insisted they were "definitely real voices in [her] head." Foundation monitoring indicated that Subject B had spent several evenings discussing Subject C's condition before developing symptoms herself.

Addendum 2953-3: Transmission Experiment

Under controlled conditions, D-Class subject D-7294 was exposed to audio recordings of an SCP-2953 affected individual describing their perceived symptoms for 45 minutes. Within 12 hours, D-7294 began reporting that he was "hearing voices" despite no observable indications of hallucinatory experiences. When staff intentionally ignored his claims, D-7294 began to make more dramatic statements about his "symptoms," eventually describing elaborate conspiracy theories that he claimed were his delusions. Cognitive testing showed normal thought processes, and D-7294 was able to engage in complex reasoning tasks without difficulty ‚Äì a capability typically impaired in actual schizophrenic disorder.

This experiment confirmed that SCP-2953 can be transmitted through recorded media, not just direct interpersonal contact, significantly complicating containment efforts.

Addendum 2953-4: Incident 2953-A

On ‚ñà‚ñà/‚ñà‚ñà/20‚ñà‚ñà, three Level 2 researchers assigned to documentation of SCP-2953 reported to the medical wing with symptoms consistent with SCP-2953 infection. Analysis by the Memetics Department confirmed that the SCP-2953 documentation itself had become a vector for transmission, particularly the detailed case studies and symptom descriptions. This represents the first confirmed case of an SCP file itself becoming capable of transmitting the anomalous effect it documents.

Addendum 2953-5: SPECIAL ACCESS RESTRICTIONS

Following Incident 2953-A, this document has been designated a Category-3 Memetic Hazard. The following access protocols are now in effect:

  1. This document must be accessed in segments, with mandatory 24-hour cognitive reset periods between reading each section.
  2. All personnel accessing this document must undergo Pre-Exposure Cognitive Anchoring (PECA) therapy, which includes reinforcement of reality testing skills and installation of specific cognitive firewalls.
  3. A maximum of 15 minutes viewing time per segment is permitted.
  4. Post-exposure evaluation is mandatory within 6 hours of document access.
